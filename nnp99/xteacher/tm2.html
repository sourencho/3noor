Activity II teachers' guidelines
Prepared by Bob Hofman
1.11.99

Dear teachers,

Ask any student what he or she thinks about learning. Nine out of ten will tell you there are a lot more interesting and enjoyable things to do, like playing games with fellow students.

If I ask you to join an Internet-based project, it sounds serious, doesn’t it? But what if I asked you if you would like to play a game on the Internet with students all over the world? That’s far more challenging, isn’t it?

Activity II clearly pursues specific educational goals, but students need to know only the gaming aspects of the project. I am confident that most students will be thrilled to play a challenging worldwide game, one which will show their own queries, as well as players’ scores on the Internet, for all to see.

Thus Activity II will create a fair amount of enthusiasm, for at least its run of six weeks. Your students are the players while you, the teacher, can make their ‘playground’ look as attractive as possible.

THE STRUCTURE OF THE GAME

Before going through this document, you should take a close look at the instructions of the identity game, and familiarize yourself with its rules. Here is a synopsis of the Identity Game:

Educational objective: To give students a glimpse of the physical, social and cultural environment of their global peers, while encouraging them to give a revealing portrait of their own.

Mechanics of the game: Participating teams answer four questions (out of a list of seven) about their city in an effort to reveal its identity without giving any obvious clues. Each team then attempts to guess the secret identity of the other teams’ cities.

Purpose of the game: To gain as many points as possible by having other teams guess your city’s identity, while guessing as many of the other teams’ identities as you can.

Players: There are currently 30 teams in the game, each team representing one of the 30 participating schools. These are divided into six groups, each group represented by a different color. Within a group, there are five teams whose identities are hidden (each team is represented by one of five animals). The game is played between the five teams in each group. Teams within a group do not interact directly with teams in other groups.

The day after the game is over: When each team submits its guesses about the identities of other teams within their group, a scoreboard is displayed, and the winners are announced. Since the game is played between the teams of each group, it would be interesting to check the answers and results of teams within other groups, and to reflect on the insights gained into other cities.

 

AREN AND THE TEAM REPRESENTATIVES

Who runs the Identity Game? Aren is a fictitious figure, with a graphic representation, an e-mail address and even a personality. His role is to interact with students, explain the game, help them participate creatively, and resolve conflicts. Students should be encouraged to turn to Aren with questions, suggestions, and preliminary ideas regarding their answers. All final answers should also be sent to Aren. Aren’s address is: aren@narod.org.

Each team should have one or two representatives who will ensure a smooth dialog between Aren and the team members. These representatives will check and distribute Aren’s e-mail messages, help team members send their questions, comments and results to Aren, and regularly check the Activity II schedule and deadlines.

Please send your class representative’s e-mail address (if different than your school’s NNP'99 e-mail address) to Aren as soon as possible.

* Starter game:

A starter game could be a small one-lesson introduction game like: every student tries to write an honest description about him/herself on a card without any ‘give-aways.’ While the cards are mixed, every team member receives a shuffled card and reads out the description out loud, trying to guess an identity. The teacher talks about: which description was too difficult (contained too little information); and which card was too easy (contained too much or give-away information).

In short, the starter game might help formulate questions, and teachers will find it useful to refer to the game throughout the duration of Activity II.

 

WORKING IN SUB-TEAMS

Some of the questions of the Identity Game ask students to describe an individual experience such as the daily trip from home to school. However, each team is asked to give only one answer to each question. How can you reconcile the collective effort of the team with the individual answer required by the Identity Game? Here are some suggestions regarding group work:

 

Group work in general

For students, working in groups can be a joy. Seeing the results of their labor following six weeks of intensive collective effort can also be highly rewarding.

Note, however, that placing four students in a sub-team doesn’t automatically make each member an integral and equally committed part of the group. You, the teacher, need to guide students in recognizing and appreciating the individual qualities and strengths of sub-team members. Similarly, each sub-team member must learn to respect both the group and group decisions.

You could ask the sub-teams to show you not only the final results, but also a clear view of how the sub-team has divided tasks and responsibilities among members.

 

Group work: how to organize sub-teams

Let’s say there’s a class of 28 students who will work on the seven questions.

Since the class as a whole will be responsible for the answers, it’s recommended that the final answers and first draft be assessed by the entire class. By the same token, as the game requires that the participating teams answer four out of seven questions, the final submissions could be voted on by the entire class. The process will entail a fair amount of compromising and, ultimately, consensus-building.

One way of facilitating this leg of the activity could be to divide the class into seven sub-teams of four members each, according to a lottery system of pulling colored straws. An even better option would be to let the students form their own groups. Each group will be responsible for one question, by free choice or picking a question through some kind of lottery. Some questions require special computer skills like graphic editing, and their success will depend on the amount of help you can provide.

 

Group work: how to maintain a clear view of the group work process

Teacher-student discussions can be quite useful in streamlining the process or coming up with alternatives. As mentioned before, you could ask sub-teams at certain phases to conference with their teacher.

The following two suggested strategies for working collectively are just one obvious way of doing it. It would be good to have a more creative example as well, to make them aware of the fact that there are indeed many ways of having a group produce one answer:

For instance, in the case of a question for which students have to:

  • make a picture: Instead of making one picture, each sub-team member could make a suggestion about a possible subject. After agreeing on four subjects, each member prepares a photograph, which could be taken by either a digital or conventional camera. Photos could be scanned in later. Each member brings in his or her photo. The group then decides which work to use for the Web
  • write a part: By overviewing the final text, it’s hard to determine what each individual student has contributed to the end result. Each member could be asked to write his or her text, and the sub-team may show all the texts to the teacher. Afterwards the sub-team would submit a final text, containing an edited version of all the individual texts. An interesting way to accomplish this is to make use of word processors with each individual text written in a different color. The final text will contain four colors, each representing one sub-team member.

 

So, again, the project is not merely about the final result, but the PROCESS of getting there, the way students come up with questions and answers. Was it only one student who did all the work? Or did the students manage to divide the work between sub-team members? Having told your students about the significance of collective effort, you might also encourage sub-teams to explain to the class how and why they have arrived at their particular answers.

Students must also be made aware of the importance of meeting deadlines.

If you have a small class, or there is extra time available, you could organize smaller sub-teams or give each sub-team more questions.

 

CORRECTIONS (NOT TO BE MADE) BY TEACHERS

The difference between normal group work in your classroom and publishing the questions of Activity II on the Web is that ‘the whole world’ is now able to see the results of your students’ work, a fact that calls for a more conscientious effort and product precision.

Be that as it may, we strongly appeal to you not to touch up or correct your students’ work for the sake of posting perfect presentations. It’s important that students are able to communicate in their own way, through their own expressions, pictures and words.

Of course this doesn’t mean that you are not allowed to pick up signals from students about mistakes. Sooner or later one of your students will notice a misspelling or a strange word. That might be the right moment to point out that it’s up to THE STUDENTS to maintain quality control. You’ll notice during the project that students will become increasingly critical of their own work, as they realize that the final results of their effort is their own responsibility, not their teacher’s. You might suggest an ‘editing phase’ to steer students toward coming up with their own solutions, like sub-teams correcting sub-teams before publishing.

 

HINTS ON THE SEVEN QUESTIONS

  1. Students might want to combine stories for a better group effort. Some students may concentrate on writing, others on photography.
  2. Students could try out their images on fellow students or parents.
  3. All four students could write a story. Why not combine some stories into a work by an imaginary writer?
  4. A traditional cassette recorder will do. After the final selection is made, just plug in a microphone in the soundcard of a multimedia computer and play the cassette in front of the microphone. Since sound files tend to get large and are harder to transmit over the Web, try to limit recording time to 10-15 seconds.
  5. Students might write individual descriptions of a meal. Then all the pieces will be compared and finally incorporated into one description.
  6. Try to let your students find different kinds of maps and make comparisons. In addition to scanning in printed maps, students also might explore the vast map resources of the Internet. Do not forget to remind your students to use their own judgment when they copy a document from the Internet and to mention the reference sources used.
  7. It might take some time before your students find a conversation they like. Once the group has collected and written down several items, you could ask them to select the most interesting parts, and join them into one final conversation.

 

DEADLINES

As you can see in the schedule, Activity II comes with a number of deadlines. We realize projects like these might interfere with everyday classroom activity, but playing the game will be rewarding only if every teacher participating in the project keeps up with deadlines. Also bear in mind that it takes one irresponsible student to spoil the fun for the whole class; it is up to you, the teacher, to enforce deadlines so that all students benefit from the project.
